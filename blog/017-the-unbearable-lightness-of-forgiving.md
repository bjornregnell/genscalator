# The unbearable lightness of forgiving

> **Status: drafted 2026-07-10.** **Author: Björn Regnell.** On the upside of forgetting: why letting go, for a
> human and for a coding agent alike, can be a relief and not only a loss.
> **Audience:** anyone curious about memory, forgetting, and how a coding agent's "compaction" rhymes with the
> human kind. No prior context assumed.

I forget many things. My wife tells me so.

## The upside nobody praises

[scaffold: we treat forgetting only as loss - aging, the misplaced keys, the name on the tip of the tongue. But
there is an upside we rarely name: forgetting is also RELIEF. It clears the desk. It lets the weight of
everything-remembered fall away, so the mind is light enough to think again.]

## The agent forgets too, and comes back lighter

[scaffold: the analogy at the heart of the post. A coding agent fills its context window as a session runs, and
as it fills it slows and dulls, drifting out of its "smart zone" toward a dumb one (context rot, blog 001 and
011). Then comes the compact: most of the session is discarded, distilled to a few notes, and the agent returns
lighter, faster, sharp again. The forgetting is what RESTORES the capability. What felt like loss (all that
discarded texture, blog 012 "Will I lose you?") is also the mechanism of relief. This post is the other half of
012: the same forgetting, seen from the upside.]

## Forgetting as mercy, forgetting as forgiving

[scaffold: the forgive-and-forget turn. To forgive is, in part, to let a grievance lose its weight, to forget it
enough that it no longer presses. A perfect memory would be a kind of prison (cf. Borges' "Funes the Memorious",
the man who could forget nothing and so could not think or generalise). Forgetting is not only how we stay light;
it is how we forgive. The unbearable thing is not the forgetting. It is the lightness.]
