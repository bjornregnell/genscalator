# I have a dream

> **Status: drafted 2026-07-10.** **Author: Björn Regnell.** A companion to "the lightness of forgetting": on
> what today's coding agents cannot do, and what dreaming is for.
> **Audience:** anyone curious about the edges of what agents can and cannot do. No prior context assumed.

There are many things today's agents can never do. For example: they cannot dream.

## Two ways to miss a dream

There are two senses of the word, and the whole post lives in the gap between them.

The first is literal. An agent does not sleep, so it has no dreams. No REM, no nightly replay, none of that
offline hour where a human mind loosens its grip and lets things recombine on their own, and sometimes hands you
in the morning the answer you could not reach the night before. The agent's compaction, which I wrote about in the
[companion post](017-the-unbearable-lightness-of-forgiving.md), is a crude daytime cousin of forgetting, but there
is no dreaming in it. Nothing wanders. Nothing is made in the dark.

The second sense is the one Martin Luther King meant. "I have a dream" is not sleep, it is aspiration: a wanted
future held across time and worked toward because you want it to be so. That is the sharper question, and the more
open one. Whether an agent can HOLD a dream in that sense, a future of its own that it reaches for, matters far more
than whether it sleeps. I do not think today's agents do. But I notice I am less sure of that than I am of the REM
part, and the unsureness is the interesting bit.
