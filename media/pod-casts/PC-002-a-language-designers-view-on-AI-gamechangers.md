## The hook (why this episode)

For fifty years we designed programming languages so humans could tell machines what to do. Now machines write
much of the code and humans check it. What is a language even for in that world, what should it guarantee by
construction, and what must we deliberately design in so that human software engineers stay genuine authors,
in real command, rather than passengers who only click approve?

## Questions (curated from the CF5 fleet; group + trim)

### Opening: what is a language even for now
1. For fifty years we designed languages so that humans could tell machines what to do; now machines write the
   code and humans check it. If you were starting a language from a blank page today, what would you say it is
   actually for?
2. Paint us a picture of a software team ten years from now: what does the human do all day, and what does the
   language they share with their agents look like?

### Syntax, readability, and designing for human audit
3. Programmers argue endlessly about syntax, whether code looks clean or ugly on the screen. When an agent writes
   ten thousand lines before lunch, how much of that surface beauty still matters, and what takes its place?
4. Some say the future language should be easy for machines to write and easy for humans to audit, two very
   different demands. What does a language optimized for human auditing actually look like, down in the details?
5. For fifty years we designed languages for the limits of human working memory: short names, local reasoning,
   small files. Which of those old instincts should we keep, and which should we dare to throw away?

### Types, effects, and contracts: guardrail or leash
6. A type system is often sold as catching a tired programmer's mistakes. When the programmer is a tireless
   machine that makes strange mistakes instead of sloppy ones, how should we rethink what the type checker is
   guarding against?
7. A compiler error is a conversation: the language pushes back and says no. When your programmer is an agent,
   what kinds of "no" do you most want the language to be able to say, and where should those hard walls sit?
8. Effects, contracts, capabilities: we have long built ways to declare what a piece of code is allowed to touch.
   When an agent can edit anything, how do those declared boundaries change from nice documentation into
   something more like a leash?

### The verification renaissance
9. There is an old dream of provably correct software, and it always failed because proofs were too expensive for
   humans to write. If agents can now grind out the proofs overnight, what would it take to make verified
   software the normal case rather than the exotic one, and could a language lead that shift?

### Intent as a first-class thing
10. When a human writes a subtle line of code, there is intent behind it a reviewer can ask about; when an agent
    writes it, that intent lives nowhere. Could the language itself become the place where intent is recorded,
    and what would that look like?
11. When agents write most of the code, the scarce human act becomes saying precisely what we want. How should
    languages evolve to express intent, constraints, and values, rather than instructions?
12. People sometimes claim we will just talk to the machine in plain English and code becomes an invisible
    byproduct. What does English fundamentally lack that a real programming language provides?

### What matters for us human software engineers
13. For decades a programmer's identity was "I write code". If that role shifts to giving intent and judging
    results, what do we gain as engineers, and what do you fear we quietly lose?
14. Trust in software used to come from having built it yourself. What should a language give a human so they can
    honestly say "I understand this, and I stand behind it"?
15. When an AI-built system fails in production and hurts real people, who should be accountable, and what would a
    language designed for clear accountability actually look like?
16. If we stop writing code daily, our ability to read it deeply may fade, like a muscle we no longer use. How do
    we keep human comprehension alive when the practice disappears?
17. There is a real risk that humans drift from pilots to passengers in their own systems. What design choices, in
    languages or tools, would keep us in meaningful command rather than just clicking approve?
18. Many of us fell in love with programming through the joy of making something work with our own hands. Where do
    you think that joy lives in a future where the hands are mostly the machine's?

### Values, abstraction, and safety at scale
19. Languages have always encoded values: garbage collection said "programmer time matters", ownership types said
    "safety matters". Designing for the agentic era, what human values would you deliberately build in, knowing
    whatever we encode gets amplified a million times over?
20. Every abstraction in computing traded understanding for power, from assembly to compilers to frameworks. As
    agents become the next abstraction layer, where do you draw the line on what humans must still understand, and
    how can language design defend that line?
21. Mistakes used to be limited by how fast humans could type them. When agents can produce a million lines a day,
    what does safety at that scale look like, and what is the language's role compared to tests, review, and
    process?

### Teaching, the young engineer, and closers
22. Junior engineers used to grow by writing the boring code that agents now handle. Where will the next
    generation earn their scars, and what happens to a field that stops training its young?
23. Every language designer bets on what to make easy and what to make hard, and those bets shape how millions of
    people think. If agents become the main users, what is the first thing you would deliberately make harder to
    express, and why?
24. Imagine a young person starting out today who will spend her whole career working alongside agents. What
    understanding of computing will still matter deeply for her in 2040, and what should we stop teaching because
    the machines truly have it covered?
