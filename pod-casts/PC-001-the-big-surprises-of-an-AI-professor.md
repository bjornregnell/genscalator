## The hook (why this episode)

Two surprises in three years caught even the experts off guard: ChatGPT, and then, more startling to insiders,
coding agents that act on real machines. This episode asks a working AI researcher what it was like to be
repeatedly blindsided by your own field, what it broke, and whether we are any better calibrated now.

## Questions (curated from the CF5 fleet; group + trim)

### Opening: the ChatGPT moment
1. Take us back to the week ChatGPT launched: where were you when you first tried it, and when did you realize
   this was not just another demo and that your field had changed under your feet?
2. Many insiders had used GPT-3 for two years and shrugged. What changed between GPT-3 and ChatGPT that flipped
   the expert community from mild interest to shock?

### The forecasting failure
3. The core techniques (transformers, RLHF, scaling) were all published and known. What made the combination
   land so much harder than the sum of its parts?
4. Scaling laws had been in print since 2020, telling us capability would keep climbing with compute and data.
   Why did so few researchers take the curves at face value?
5. Expert surveys before 2022 put things like passing hard exams or writing working code decades away. What does
   that forecasting failure tell us about how the field builds its predictions?
6. Benchmarks were supposed to be our instruments for tracking progress, yet they gave almost no warning. Where
   did our measurement culture fail, and what should we measure instead?
7. "Emergent capabilities" became the phrase of the year. How much of that emergence is real, and how much is an
   artifact of how we measure things?

### The agentic / Claude Code second shock
8. When ChatGPT arrived the debate was about what a model could say; two years later tools like Claude Code were
   quietly rewriting files, running tests, and pushing commits. When did you first realize these systems could
   act, not just answer?
9. What is it about the step from "generates text" to "takes actions on a real machine" that made it a
   qualitatively different kind of surprise, not just more of the same scaling story?
10. Agentic coding depends on the model recovering from its own mistakes: run the code, read the error, try
    again. Why did so few of us anticipate that this feedback loop would work as well as it does?
11. How much of the agentic shock was genuinely new capability, and how much was new plumbing (tool use, long
    context, letting the model iterate) wrapped around old capability?
12. The jump from chat assistant to working coding agent took about two years, when many timelines had it a
    decade out. What does that compression tell us about how much to trust expert forecasts?
13. When a system starts editing files and running commands, the cost of an error changes completely compared to
    a wrong sentence in a chat window. How has that shifted the questions our community should be asking?
14. Can you tell us about the first time an agent did something in your own work that you honestly did not think
    the technology could do yet, and what went through your head?

### The professor's-eye and the human side
15. You spent a career building expertise, and then a system arrived that could do parts of your job. How did
    that land personally: excitement, dread, something like impostor feelings, or all of it at once?
16. Tell me about a research agenda, yours or a colleague's, that essentially died overnight. How does a
    researcher grieve a program of work, and what do you do the morning after?
17. What has this done to your students, both the PhD student wondering if their thesis will survive to the
    defense and the undergraduate wondering if what we teach will still matter in five years?
18. As teachers we built courses around the idea that writing code and writing prose are how students show
    understanding. What have you had to rebuild from the ground up, and what turned out to be worth keeping?

### Humility and calibration
19. Expert forecasts were badly wrong twice, in the same direction. What does that repeated miss tell us about
    how expertise works, and what should scientific humility look like in practice, not just as a slogan?
20. There is an odd asymmetry: the people closest to the technology were often the most dismissive of it. What is
    it about deep expertise that can make us worse at seeing what is coming, and have you caught yourself doing it
    again since?
21. If the field so badly misjudged 2022, what confidence should we place in today's predictions, and how have
    you personally changed the way you forecast: what you read, who you listen to, what evidence you weight?
22. Where do you think experts are still miscalibrated right now? Give me your best candidate for the thing that
    will make us all say, in three years, how did we not see that coming?

### Closers
23. If you were advising a 25-year-old starting a PhD in AI today, how would you tell them to choose a topic when
    the half-life of research questions has collapsed?
24. After being caught off guard twice, what does a well-calibrated AI professor look like in 2026?
