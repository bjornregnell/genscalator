## The hook (why this episode belongs in a software toolbox's podcast)

Blacksmiths live a paradox software people know by another name: **to make a tool you need a tool.** You cannot
forge a pair of tongs (**tång**) without already holding tongs to work the hot metal. Software calls this
**bootstrapping**: a compiler that compiles itself, a toolbox whose tools build the next tools, and now AI agents
that build their own tooling. genscalator is exactly this story (`tt` makes the tools that make the tools), so an
old craft that has solved the tool-bootstrapping problem for centuries has a lot to teach a new one. This episode
goes to the forge to hear how it is actually done, hands and fire, and lets the resonance with software and agents
sit underneath.

## Questions (curated; group + trim)

### The paradox itself
1. Here is the puzzle that got us to call you: you need a **tång** to make a **tång**. When you had nothing, how
   did the very first pair come to be? Walk us through it step by step.
2. The first blacksmith in history had no blacksmith's tools at all. How does a craft whose whole job is making
   tools ever get started from nothing?
3. Is there a minimum "seed kit", a handful of tools you need before you can make all the rest? What is in it?

### Making your own tools
4. Which tools does a smith make for themselves rather than buy, and why those?
5. When a job lands and you do not have the right tool, do you stop and make the tool first? Tell us about
   inventing a tool for a single, one-off task.
6. How is making a tool different from making a product for a customer: different care, different standards,
   different feeling?
7. A tool you forged shapes everything you make with it afterwards. How do you think about that leverage, that one
   good tool multiplies into a thousand better results?

### Tool quality and the work
8. How much does the quality of your tongs and hammer decide the quality of what you make? Is the tool the real
   multiplier, or is that too simple?
9. The difference between a worn or ill-fitting tool and one made exactly for your hand: how much does it change
   the work, and change your day at the forge?

### Passing it on (the apprentice angle)
10. You teach others, including BR's wife. How do you pass on tool-making itself: do apprentices forge their own
    first tools as a kind of rite of passage?
11. What do beginners get wrong about tools, and what do they only really understand after years at the anvil?

### The craft and the relationship to tools
12. What is your relationship to a tool you made yourself versus one you bought? Does a self-made tool somehow
    know your hand?
13. Is there a joy in the tool-making itself, separate from the joy of the thing the tool then helps you make?
14. Across your life, has your own toolkit kept evolving: do you keep remaking your tools as you and your work
    change?

### The bridge (BR's framing: software, compilers, AI agents)
15. In software we bootstrap too: a compiler compiles itself, and we are now building tools that build tools, even
    machines that build their own tools. Hearing that, what would you, a toolmaker in an ancient craft, want to
    tell a toolmaker in a very new one?
16. If everything were taken away tomorrow and you had only your hands and a fire, what is the first tool you would
    make, and then the next, and the next?

### Closer
17. What does a young person in any craft, forge or keyboard, most need to understand about the tools of their
    trade?
